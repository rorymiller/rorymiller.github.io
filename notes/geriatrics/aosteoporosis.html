Bisphosphonates

  • bind to bone and reduce resorptive function of osteoclasts and accelerate osteosclast apoptosis

  • specific to bone

    • 40-60% of administered dose binds to bone and remainder excreted unmetabolised in urine

  • reduce osteoporotic fractures

    • vertebral risk reduction (post-menopausal women) = 40-70%

    • hip fracture = 40-50%

  • lower fracture risk:

    • increase bone mass

    • reduce rate of bone resorption

  • BMD increase by approx. 5% following 2 years of treatment with potent bisphosphonate

  • clinical effective ness depends on:

    • bone-binding affinity

      • zolenndronic acid > alendronate > risedronate

    • level of inhibition of key enzyme

      • aolendronic acid > risedronate > alendronate

  • however; not possible as no head-head trials to state one is better than otherre reducing osteoporotic fracture risk

    • although everything better than etridronate

calcium

  • intake needs to be adequate throughout person’s life

  • 700-1000mg / day

  • calcium rich food = 300mg

    • 250mL mild

    • 40g cheese (2-3 slices cheddar)

    • half cup of tofu

    • 1 cup mussels

    • 100g canned salmon with bones

    • 1 pottle of yoghurt = 200mg

    • half cup almonds (raw) = 200mg

  • calcium supplementation should not routinely be prescribed to patients at increase risk fo fractures

    • may be required if dietary intake is insufficient

  • excessive supplementation should be avoided (>1000mg/day)

    • increase risk of kidney stones and soft-tissue calcification

  • some evidence calcium supplementation = adverse cardiovascular effects
